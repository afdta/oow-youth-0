Out of work sketches
Apr 27, 2017

Notes on strategy

Primary goal: Enable local policymakers and practitioners to tailor policies to their specific out of work populations.

Key points for web layout

  1. What does "out of work" mean?
  2. What are the "types" of out of work people (superclusters)?
  3. What are the policy options for each type?
  4. How does this play out in the U.S. or any user-selectable jurisdiction?
    • Primary point: Distribution of population among superclusters and the policy implications of that
    • Secondary: Geographic distribution of superclusters
    • Secondary: Summary statistics for each individual cluster
    • Tertiary: Detailed data [download only?]

Need to clarify

  1. How do we handle the two primary age group divisions (18-24 and 25-64)?

The out of work population
Who are they? How can policymakers and practitioners serve them best?

Most of the key points above are about describing the distribution of the population. First, in terms of status in the labor force, then in terms of superclusters, and finally in terms of individual clusters. The graphic below provides a simple way to do this, representing people with dots. As the user scrolls down the page, we can divide the groups of dots, color them differently, and annotate them to describe the various cuts of the data.

View 2: Split into employed, unemployed, not in labor force.

View 3: Split the unemployed (red) and not in labor force (blue) into "out of work." Note that these splits aren't very pretty, but they can be made to be! Rather than big long rows of dots, we can assemble them into groupings of narrower rectangles (like a treemap). We would also add legends and annotations. In this case, we would describe who is/is not included in the out of work group (e.g. unemployed HS students living at home are not "out of work").

View 4: Split the out of work into superclusters. Obviously this diagram below does not work as is. The final layout here will need to provide sufficient space to annotate with supercluster labels and perhaps bulleted lists of policy interventions.

Views 5 and 6: "The details" (no sketches yet).

View 5: subdivide the dot grid into individual clusters (15 of these). This will require some experimentation because this is a lot of detail. An alternative to the dot grid used throughout this sketch is "circle packing" which is a useful alternative to explore.

View 6: depart from the dot grid graphic and display a series of small multiple maps that show the geographic distribution of each supercluster. Imagine 9 small maps in a 3x3 grid to give the user a quick sense for where, say, most of the middle-aged/HS or less supercluster lives

View 7: "Soooo much detail"

We probably don't need to visualize this, rather provide a way to download the full suite of indicators for each jurisdiction.

Thoughts/Questions

The above sketches/notes don't make clear how we orient the user to the concept of the super cluster while conveying the policy implications of each. Redefining global cities provides one model for doing that.
